Overview
Do our music choices really reflect how we feel?

This project aims to reveal how culture, emotion, and geography intersect through sound. Music can be both a mirror and a remedy for our mental state, but what is it more often? By analyzing these relationships, we want to visualize music not just as entertainment, but as a powerful and meaningful part of mental health.

We focused our analysis around 3 big questions: What music genres are in connection with poorer mental health? What music genres are in connection with better mental health? What countries listen to the genres that are in connection with better mental health?

Our analysis is inspired by studies on how music can play a role in our mood regulation

This article explains how there is an intersection between music and mental-health and that music isn’t just entertainment, it also has a deeper meaning and influence on people. Our project compares music popularity by genre in each country and each genre’s intersection with mental-health.

It is also inspired by investigations on how music can have broad physiological and psychological benefits.

This is in relation to exploring music’s impact on mental health. This study emphasizes the clinical and biological effects of music. This does not highlight music genre popularity by nation. Ethical Considerations

When analyzing datasets that combine mental health statistics and music genre preferences, several ethical considerations and limitations must be addressed. Mental health data is sensitive, so protecting privacy and ensuring individuals cannot be identified is important. Because the data is often self-reported, responses may not always accurately reflect clinical conditions, and results may be influenced by participants’ moods or interpretations of survey questions. Researchers must also be careful to avoid reinforcing stereotypes about listeners of certain genres and recognize that survey data may include sampling bias, meaning the results may not represent the entire population.

Data Context
Dataset 1: Mental Health Background

The dataset focuses on mental health outcomes and system indicators across OECD countries, including the anxiety and depression symptoms, suicide rates, and changes in population mental health before and after the COVID-19 pandemic. It is compelling to analyze because it captures how large-scale social and economic disruptions affect mental well-being, allows for cross-national comparisons using comparable indicators, and directly connects mental health outcomes to policy responses and health system capacity. While the dataset is rich on a national level, it could still be strengthened by including more breakdowns on demographics and more time-series data to track changes over time, ensuring the data is being updated over time. Adding these factors would allow for deeper analysis and a more complete understanding of what leads to mental health differences between countries.

About the Data The data comes from the OECD Health at a Glance 2023 report, specifically the Mental Health chapter of the report published by the OECD in November 2023. The full report and mental health figures can be downloaded as a PDF from the OECD website.

The report does not provide one unified table with a fixed number of rows, because it is a compiled report containing multiple figures and tables. In general, the mental health indicators cover OECD members roughly 30–38 countries, often across multiple years, such as 2019–2022. Similarly, the number of columns depends on which indicator table is used from the report or its underlying OECD data. Typical tables include 5–10 features, such as country name, year, and one or more mental health indicators. Common columns include measures like prevalence of anxiety or depression symptoms and suicide mortality rates, sometimes with additional breakdowns. Many features in this dataset also appear in other public health and international datasets, including geographic variables, time variables, and mental health indicators such as prevalence of depression or anxiety and suicide rates.

Dataset 2: Music Genres Background

The dataset addresses the most popular music genres in different countries using listening data from the Spotify API. It is also strong to analyze because it contains cross-country comparisons of music preferences, highlights cultural differences in entertainment consumption, and connects global streaming behavior to regional trends in music. These factors provide clear information on this dataset and help researchers to analyze the data more easily. While the dataset offers a clear overview of genre popularity at the country level, it could also be strengthened by including demographic breakdowns such as age or gender, as well as additional time-based data to show how preferences change over time.

About the Data The data came from searching Google Datasets. The dataset is available for download on Kaggle. The data was taken from Spotify’s Top 50 Playlists (by country).

The report contains observations from 73 countries (countries that Spotify covered in their top 50s). The report also contains 6 columns grouping different music genres. The creator of the dataset noted that the sub genres pulled from Spotify were grouped into each of the 6 main genres provided. Features in this dataset such as country, and music genres can be found in other datasets.

What music genres are in connection with poorer mental health?

Latin Reggaeton shows a negative relationship with suicide rates. Countries with higher popularity of this genre tend to have lower suicide rates.

What music genres are in connection with better mental health?

Pop music shows a slight positive relationship, meaning suicide rates appear to increase slightly as pop popularity increases. EDM appears to show a negative relationship, indicating depression rates may decrease as EDM popularity increases.

What countries listen to the genres that are in connection with better mental health?

Countries such as Colombia, Mexico, Chile, and Spain have the highest popularity of Latin Reggaeton. These countries also show better (lower) suicide rate scores compared to others in the dataset. Many European countries show very low popularity of this genre.

Conclusion

Our analysis explored whether there is a relationship between music genre preferences and mental health outcomes. One major takeaway was that some music genres appeared more frequently among individuals reporting poorer mental health indicators such as higher stress or anxiety. However, this does not mean the music causes these feelings. Instead, it may suggest that people choose music that reflects or helps them process their emotions. Another takeaway was that some genres were more commonly associated with better reported mental health, which may indicate that certain types of music are used for relaxation or mood regulation.

We also observed geographic patterns in music preferences. Some countries tended to listen more to genres that were associated with better mental health responses in our dataset. This suggests that culture and location may influence both music taste and how people use music to support their emotional well-being. However, these patterns should be interpreted cautiously because music popularity is influenced by many factors beyond mental health.

Overall, the graphs suggest that there may be some associations between music genre popularity and mental health indicators, but the relationships are generally weak and inconsistent. Certain genres show slight patterns, such as Latin Reggaeton being associated with lower suicide rates and pop music being associated with lower depression and anxiety rates. However, the scattered data points indicate that music preferences alone are unlikely to be a major factor influencing mental health outcomes. Other cultural, economic, and social factors likely play a much larger role.

Therefore, while music may contribute to emotional expression and coping, the data suggests that no single genre strongly predicts mental health trends across countries. Our analysis may overestimate or underestimate the true relationship between music and mental health because the data relies on self-reported survey responses and may not represent all populations. In the future, research could use larger and more diverse datasets and include additional factors such as age, listening habits, and listening context to better understand how music preferences relate to emotional well-being.
